Understanding GROSS PROFIT

The Cost of Sales Formula

Opening Stock
+ Purchases
- Closing Stock
= Cost of Goods Sold

üçû Bakery Example - Why This Formula Works

Monday morning (Opening Stock): 50 loaves in store

Monday deliveries (Purchases): 200 new loaves arrive

Available to sell: 50 + 200 = 250 loaves

Monday night (Closing Stock): 30 loaves left

How many sold?

250 available - 30 left = 220 loaves SOLD!

This is Cost of Sales = What was actually sold to customers

üí° WHY ADD Opening Stock?

Because you had it available to sell! It could have been sold this year.

üí° WHY SUBTRACT Closing Stock?

Because it's still in the warehouse! It WASN'T sold to customers yet, so it's not a cost this year.

üìö TEXTBOOK FORMULA

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) = Opening Inventory + Purchases - Closing Inventory

Gross Profit = Revenue - COGS

SLIDE 09 / 20

The ACCOUNTING EQUATION

ASSETS = LIABILITIES + EQUITY

This MUST ALWAYS balance!

Why does it balance?

Everything a business owns (assets) is funded by either:

‚úì Liabilities - Money borrowed from others (loans, suppliers)

‚úì Equity - Money from owners (investment + retained profits)

Assets are FUNDED BY liabilities and equity!

üè¢ Office Building Example

Company buys office for ¬£500,000

How did they pay for it?

Bank loan: ¬£350,000 (Liability)

Owner's money: ¬£150,000 (Equity)

Check the equation:

Assets (¬£500k building) = Liabilities (¬£350k loan) + Equity (¬£150k owner's money)

¬£500,000 = ¬£350,000 + ¬£150,000 ‚úì

It balances! That's why it's called a Balance Sheet!

üìö KEY PRINCIPLE

The accounting equation MUST always balance. If it doesn't, there's an error in the accounts!

PROFIT vs CASH - The Critical Difference

The ¬£10,000 Credit Sale Example

March sale ‚Üí April payment

üìÖ Timeline of Events

March 15th: Company sells goods for ¬£10,000 on credit

Customer promises to pay in 30 days (April 15th)

Question 1: Does PROFIT increase in March?

‚úì YES! Profit +¬£10,000 in MARCH

Why? Accounting recognizes revenue when EARNED (sale made), not when cash received!

Question 2: Does CASH increase in March?

‚úó NO! Cash +¬£0 in MARCH

Why? Customer hasn't paid yet! No actual money received!

April 15th: Customer pays ¬£10,000

Profit in April: ¬£0 (already counted in March)

Cash in April: +¬£10,000 ‚úì

Income Statement (March): Shows ¬£10k profit

Cash Flow Statement (March): Shows ¬£0 cash increase

This is the TIMING DIFFERENCE!

üí° WHY This Matters

A company can show huge profits on the income statement but run out of cash and go BANKRUPT!

This is why investors read BOTH statements - profit tells one story, cash tells another!

üìö ACCRUAL ACCOUNTING

Income Statement uses "accrual accounting" - records revenue when earned, not when cash received.

Cash Flow Statement shows actual cash movements - when money physically changes hands.
